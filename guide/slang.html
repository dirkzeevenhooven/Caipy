Cape Town Guide · Local Culture

Speak Like a
Local

South Africans have a language of their own — a glorious mix of Afrikaans, Zulu, Xhosa, English, and pure street creativity. Learn a few words and you'll instantly connect with locals in a way no tourist ever does.

Don't be shy about using them. Capetonians love it when visitors make the effort — and they'll correct you with a smile if you get it wrong.

The Essential 8 — learn these before you land

If you only remember eight words, make it these.

  • Howzit — Hello / How are you?
  • Lekker — Nice, great, delicious, cool
  • Ja — Yes (sounds like "yah")
  • Sharp sharp — OK, agreed, no problem
  • Braai — BBQ (sacred institution)
  • Just now — Later (not now, not soon)
  • Eish — Wow / Oh no / That's rough
  • Robot — Traffic light (not a machine)

Greetings & Everyday

How to say hello, yes, and everything in between.

Howzit
HOW-zit
Universal greeting meaning "How are you?" or simply "Hello." Used constantly, by everyone, in every context.
"Howzit bru, how's it going?"
Ja
Yah
Yes. From Afrikaans. You'll hear this instead of "yes" in almost every conversation.
"Ja, that braai was lekker hey."
Sharp sharp
Sharp sharp
OK, agreed, no problem, sounds good. One "sharp" works too, but doubling it adds enthusiasm.
"Meet at 6?" — "Sharp sharp."
Lekker
LECK-er
Nice, great, delicious, cool, enjoyable. The single most useful word in the Cape Town vocabulary. Use it for everything.
"That was a lekker day hey."
Eish
Aysh
A versatile exclamation of surprise, dismay, disbelief or sympathy. From Zulu and Xhosa. Works in almost any situation.
"Eish, that traffic was bad."
Ag
Ach
A sigh or mild expression — like "oh" or "ah." Can signal resignation, mild irritation, or warmth depending on tone.
"Ag, don't worry about it."
Yebo
YEH-boh
Yes, in Zulu. Widely understood across South Africa. Often used enthusiastically.
"You coming to the braai?" — "Yebo!"
Sommer
SOM-er
Just because, randomly, without a specific reason. A very Afrikaans concept — doing something for the sheer sake of it.
"We just went for a drive sommer."
Dirk's Tip

The fastest way to make a local smile: greet them with "Howzit" and end any compliment with "lekker." Works in a restaurant, at a market, on a hike — anywhere. They'll know immediately you're paying attention.

South African Time

This one will confuse you. Learn it early.

Now now
Now now
Soon — but not immediately. Could be 5 minutes, could be 30. Context-dependent. More urgent than "just now."
"I'll be there now now."
Just now
Just now
Later. Not now. Possibly much later. The most misunderstood phrase for visitors — it does NOT mean immediately.
"I'll do it just now." (could be hours)
Now
Now
Actually means now. The only one of the three that means what it says — but locals rarely use it alone.
"Come now!" (means immediately)
Dirk's Tip

Don't get frustrated by "just now." It's not rudeness — it's a genuine cultural relationship with time that locals are proud of. If something is truly urgent, say "right now" or "immediately." Otherwise, relax — you're in Cape Town.

People & Places

Who's who and what things are called.

Bru / Boet
Brew / Boot
Bro, mate, friend. "Bru" is Cape Town coloured community slang that's gone mainstream. "Boet" is Afrikaans for brother, same warmth.
"My bru, you have to try the oysters."
Oke / Ou
Oak / Oh
A guy, bloke, person. Completely neutral. "That oke over there" just means "that person."
"He's a good oke, very chilled."
China
CHY-na
Friend, mate. Originally from Cockney rhyming slang (china plate = mate). Common in Cape Town street culture.
"He's my china, known him for years."
Mzansi
mm-ZAN-see
South Africa — the informal, proud name for the country. From the Xhosa word for "south." Used constantly in casual speech.
"Mzansi is the most beautiful country."
Robot
ROH-bot
Traffic light. Not a machine. This will catch you off guard. "Turn left at the robot" is completely normal.
"Turn right at the second robot."
Bakkie
BUCK-ee
A pickup truck. Very common vehicle in South Africa, very common word. If someone offers you a lift in their bakkie, it's a pickup.
"He drove it over in his bakkie."
Spaza
SPA-za
A small informal shop, usually home-based, selling basics like bread, airtime, and snacks. The township convenience store.
"Grab some water from the spaza."
Sangoma
san-GOH-ma
A traditional healer — part herbalist, part spiritual practitioner. A deeply respected figure in African culture. Not to be confused with a witch doctor.
"She consulted a sangoma before the decision."

Food, Drink & Braai

The most important vocabulary in the country.

Braai
Bry (rhymes with "cry")
A barbecue — but so much more than that. It's a social ritual, a cultural institution, a reason to gather. South Africans braai year-round, in any weather.
"We're having a braai on Sunday, join us."
Boerewors
BOO-reh-vors
A spiced coiled sausage — the centrepiece of every braai. Made from beef, pork and spices. "Wors" for short. Non-negotiable at any braai.
"Throw on some wors, the braai's ready."
Biltong
BIL-tong
Dried, cured meat — the South African answer to jerky but infinitely better. Beef, game or ostrich. Buy it everywhere. Eat it constantly.
"Pack some biltong for the hike."
Dop
Dop
A drink — specifically an alcoholic one. "Having a dop" means having a drink. "Dop" can also mean to fail an exam, but in social settings it's always about the drink.
"Fancy a dop after work?"
Rooibos
ROY-bos
Red bush herbal tea — grown only in the Cederberg mountains near Cape Town. South Africa's most famous export. Ask for it everywhere.
"Can I get a rooibos, no milk."
Vetkoek
FET-cook
Deep-fried dough bread filled with mince, jam, or cheese. A South African street food classic. The name literally means "fat cake."
"Get a vetkoek from the market stall."
Potjie
POY-kee
A slow-cooked stew made in a cast-iron pot over coals. A weekend ritual — the slower the better. Never stir a potjie. Ever.
"The potjie's been on since this morning."
Naartjie
NAR-chee
A tangerine or mandarin. From the Cape Malay community. You'll see them sold at markets and roadside stalls all over the Western Cape.
"Grab a bag of naartjies at the market."
Dirk's Tip

If a local invites you to a braai, say yes. Always say yes. Bring something to drink, offer to help with the fire, and never rush. A braai is not a quick meal — it's an afternoon. It's one of the best ways to experience real South African culture.

Expressions & Feelings

What South Africans say when they feel something.

Shame
Shame
This one surprises visitors the most. "Shame" is used to express cuteness, sympathy, or endearment — not embarrassment. A puppy is "so shame." A sick child is "shame, poor thing."
"Look at that puppy — shame, so cute!"
Hey
Hey
Added to the end of sentences for emphasis or to invite agreement. A uniquely South African verbal tic you'll pick up within hours of arriving.
"That hike was hectic hey."
Jol
Joll
A party, a fun event, a good time. Also a verb — to jol means to party or go out. Cape Town has excellent jolling options.
"What a jol that was last night."
Hectic
HEC-tic
Intense, extreme, or impressive — not necessarily negative. A hectic view means an incredible view. Hectic traffic means very bad traffic. Context is everything.
"The sunset from Lion's Head was hectic."
Gatvol
GHUT-fol
Fed up, done, had enough. Literally "full of hole" in Afrikaans. A very expressive way of saying you're completely over something.
"I'm gatvol of this traffic, eish."
Ubuntu
oo-BOON-too
A Zulu/Xhosa philosophy meaning "I am because we are." Humanity, community, collective care. One of South Africa's most important and beautiful concepts.
"Ubuntu — that's why the community helped."
Skinner
SKIN-er
Gossip, or to gossip. "What's the skinner?" means "what's the latest gossip?" A beloved pastime.
"Eish, the skinner at work today was bad."
Kak
Kuck
Rubbish, bad, nonsense. A mild expletive by South African standards, used constantly in casual conversation. "That's kak" means "that's rubbish."
"The weather today is kak."

Cape Town Specific

Words and phrases you'll only really hear in the Mother City.

Kaap
Karp
Afrikaans for "Cape" — as in Cape Town. Locals sometimes call the city "die Kaap" (the Cape). A term of affection.
"Nothing beats die Kaap in summer."
Bo-Kaap
Boo-Karp
Literally "above the Cape" — the colourful Malay Quarter on the slopes of Signal Hill. One of Cape Town's most photographed neighbourhoods. Home of Cape Malay culture.
"Take a walk through Bo-Kaap at sunrise."
The Berg
The Berg
Table Mountain. Locals don't usually call it by its full name — it's "the Berg" (the mountain) or simply "the mountain." A constant reference point in daily life.
"You can see the Berg from here."
Koektail
COOK-tail
Cookie / biscuit in Afrikaans. You'll see this on menus and in bakeries. Cape Town has a strong Afrikaans baking culture — the koektail is part of it.
"Two koektail with the coffee, please."
Tackies
TACK-eez
Sneakers, trainers. Wear tackies on the mountain. Essential vocabulary for any outdoor activity in Cape Town.
"Wear your old tackies for the hike."
Trek
Trek
To pull, or a long difficult journey. Also means to feel like doing something: "I don't have the trek" means "I can't be bothered." Used in English conversation all the time.
"I don't have the trek to walk there."
Dirk's Tip

Cape Town's coloured community has its own rich dialect — a blend of Afrikaans, English and Malay that's unlike anything else in the world. The words "bru," "china," and "lekker" all flow from this culture. When you use them, you're connecting with something deep and uniquely Cape Town.
