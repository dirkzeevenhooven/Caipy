Planet Curated · Cape Town

Cape Town Through a Lens

Cape Town is one of the most photogenic cities on earth. Golden light, dramatic mountains, colourful streets, wild coastlines. These are the spots that reward you — and the times to be there.

Golden Hour Spots

Signal Hill · Sunrise
Signal Hill at Dawn
Be there by 6am

Empty roads, Table Mountain glowing in the first light, the city laid out below you. Signal Hill at sunrise is as close to having Cape Town to yourself as you'll get. The elevated angle gives you the full panorama — mountain, city, ocean — all in one frame. Don't be late; the light moves fast.

Camps Bay · Sunset
Camps Bay Beach
Arrive 30 min before sunset

The Twelve Apostles mountain range turns deep orange, then crimson, as the sun drops behind the Atlantic. Position yourself on the beach or the promenade looking back at the mountains — the light is extraordinary. Arrive 30 minutes early to claim your spot and feel the scene settle.

Bo-Kaap · Dawn
Bo-Kaap Cobbled Streets
Go before 8am

Before the tourist groups arrive, Bo-Kaap is a photographer's dream. Cobblestoned streets, vibrantly painted houses, soft morning light raking across the facades. The silence of early morning lets you compose properly. Go before 8am on a weekday and you'll have entire streets to yourself.

Chapman's Peak · Mid-morning
Chapman's Peak Lookout
Best mid-morning

One of the most dramatic coastal viewpoints in the world. The cliffs drop sharply into the Atlantic below, and the winding Chapman's Peak Drive snakes its way along the rock face. Mid-morning gives you warm directional light without the harsh midday contrast. A wide angle lens earns its keep here.

Simon's Town · Any time
Boulders Beach Penguins
Overcast days give the softest light

African penguins against rounded granite boulders — it works in almost any light, but overcast days are the secret. Clouds act as a giant diffuser, eliminating harsh shadows and letting the textures of the rocks and the penguins' markings come through cleanly. Arrive at opening time to get close before the crowds build.

Hidden Photo Spots

Muizenberg · Sunrise
Muizenberg Beach Huts
No crowds before 8am

The iconic row of candy-coloured changing rooms at Muizenberg is one of Cape Town's most photographed sights — but most people see it with a crowd. Arrive at sunrise and you'll find the huts bathed in warm light, the beach empty, and the whole scene completely yours. The low morning sun creates long shadows that add real drama.

Woodstock · Street photography
Old Biscuit Mill Doorways
Any time

The side streets and alleyways around the Old Biscuit Mill in Woodstock are covered in murals, textured walls, and industrial character that most visitors never find. This is Cape Town's creative district at its rawest — faded paint, geometric street art, and doorways that look like they were designed to be photographed.

Gardens · Hidden view
De Waal Park Looking Up
Morning or late afternoon

Stand in De Waal Park and look up towards Devil's Peak — the contrast of the leafy Victorian park in the foreground against the raw mountain behind is a composition you rarely see in Cape Town photography. It's the city meets wilderness in a single frame. Quiet, beautiful, and almost always overlooked.

Kalk Bay · Dawn
Kalk Bay Harbour at Dawn
Working boats come in early morning

The working fishing boats return to Kalk Bay harbour in the early hours, and the scene — weathered hulls, seabirds, fishermen unloading the catch against the morning sky — is genuinely documentary. It feels like another era. Bring a fast lens if the light is low; the movement of the boats rewards patience.

Practical Tips

☀️
Shoot the edges of the day

Cape Town's midday light is intense and flat — the mountains wash out and the ocean glares. The magic happens in the first and last two hours of daylight. Plan your schedule around that.

💨
The Cape Doctor is real

Cape Town's famous south-easterly wind can shake your frame on a long exposure. Bring a tripod if you're shooting waterfalls, seascapes, or night work. On windy days, lower angles and wider apertures help.

🚡
Table Mountain Cable Car — go late

Take the last cable car of the afternoon. The rotating cabin gives you 360° of Cape Town in golden light, and the views from the top at sunset are extraordinary. Book ahead — it fills up fast in summer.

🤝
Ask before you photograph people

Always ask permission before photographing people in informal settlements or in communities you're passing through. A genuine connection makes for a better portrait anyway — and it's the right thing to do.

🚁
Drone rules in Cape Town

You need a SACAA licence to fly commercially in South Africa. Recreational drones are permitted in most open areas outside controlled airspace — but always check the map before you launch. Signal Hill and Chapman's Peak are worth checking in advance.

Gear Tips

🔭
Wide angle — non-negotiable

Cape Town's mountain-meets-city compositions are built for wide glass. A 16–35mm or 24mm prime will earn its weight on every single day you're here. The scale of Table Mountain demands it.

🐧
A 70–200mm for wildlife

Penguins at Boulders, whales off Hermanus, Cape fur seals at Hout Bay — a telephoto zoom lets you compress the distance and fill the frame. Essential if you're combining wildlife with landscapes on the same trip.

🌊
ND filter for water

The waterfalls near Hout Bay and the tidal pools at Kalk Bay reward long exposures — silk-smooth water against sharp rock. A 6-stop ND filter gives you that in bright conditions. Don't leave it at home.

🎒
Waterproof your bag

Sea spray at Boulders Beach and Llandudno is genuinely aggressive. A waterproof bag or at minimum a rain cover is essential — not optional. Salt air will find any gap you leave open.
